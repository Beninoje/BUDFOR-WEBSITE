How To Make (And Stick To) A Budget Amid A Looming Recession

15 MIN READ |

APR 15, 2023

On This Page

IMAGE: PIXABAY.COM/VICTORIA_WATERCOLOR

With inflation on the rise and an unpredictable economy, having a reliable budget is arguably more important than ever. But is it really all that difficult?

The short answer is no. It is not really that difficult to create a solid budget, the problem comes later — sticking to the budget over time. But that's ok, we're here to guide you through it! Follow the steps below and make sure to read all the way to the end for some gritty advice.

What, Technically, Is A Recession?

A recession is a large and often consistent period of economic decline that can last for months, or even years at a time. They often result in low consumer activity, less products and services, and ultimately widespread unemployment.[1] Establishing a budget amid a recession typically means adopting a more frugal lifestyle. This means more saving, less spending, and smarter consumer choices.

Step 1: Calculate Your Income Streams

Knowing your income streams forms the foundation of any budget. It essentially tells you how much 'room' you have to work with for allocating expenses. An income stream is any net positive supply of money that comes from something such as a job, a business, or an investment. To begin your budget, record all income streams along with their estimated value.

Step 2: Locate Any Savings

Reviewing your portfolio at every financial institution you hold funds in can prove beneficial. Maybe you've forgotten about that GIC you purchased a few years back. Ok, you may not get that lucky but it's worth a shot reviewing your accounts every so often. Add any and all savings to your budget sheet, including the amount and domicile of the funds. These may include savings accounts, investment accounts such as a TFSA, or any cash you may have on hand.

Step 3: Track Your Spending

Next, tracking your spending is arguably the most vital part of creating a successful budget. Everything from your morning coffee to your monthly grocery run should be counted. You may also wish to further break down your expenses into categories and subcategories for a more straightforward analysis. When reflecting on your spending, don't forget to factor in the 50/30/20 budgeting rule.[2]

Step 4: Build Your Budget

Finally, and maybe you've been doing it all along, it's time to put everything into a formal budget. Whether using Microsoft Excel or Google Sheets, your budget should be simple and concise.

Step 5: Review & Consider The Next Steps

Most experts recommend reviewing your budget near the end of each month. This allows you to update and pivot for the coming months.[3] During the review stage, consider the future and note any significant expenses that may be coming.

Tips & Tricks For Sticking To A Budget

  • For every purchase, take a picture of the receipt and/or store it in a safe place
  • If anything changes with your finances (e.g. a pay raise), update the information in your budget immediately
  • Take time to think over large purchases, most often you will find that it's something you don't truly need
  • Last but not least, don't spend more than you can afford

4 Types of Banks

Commercial Banks

Commercial Banks generally have teller, sales associates, trust officers, loan officers, branch managers, and tech specialists. These banks offer loans, take deposits and provide other banking services.

Cooperative Banks

Cooperative banking institutions take deposits and lend money. They are carried out by credit unions, mutual saving banks, mutual organizations

Specialized Banks

Specialized Banks are banks that offer a specific type of economic activity such as foreign trading, industrial activity, agricultural, mortgage, rural development, loans, and deposits.

Central Banks

Central Banks is a financial institution that control the production and distribution of money and credit for a certain nation. They are responsible for monitoring all the activities of the commercial banks.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a Mortgage?

A mortgage is a loan provided by a bank or other lender to help you purchase a home. You'll pay back the loan over a set period of time, usually with interest.

What is a "Crossed Cheque"?

A Crossed Cheque account refers to the amount that should be deposited into a payees(a person or organization that receives payment for goods or services) account and cannot be cashed over the counter.

What is Meant By Prime Rate?

The prime rate is an annual interest rate that commercial banks and financial institutions use to set interest rates for variable loans and lines of credit. It is influenced based on the interest rate set by the Bank Of Canada.

Why is it Important to choose a Bank that is a Member of the CDIC?

Choosing a bank that is a member of the CDIC (Canada Deposit Insurance Corporation) can help protect your deposits in the event that your bank fails. The CDIC provides insurance on eligible deposits up to $100,000 per depositor per insured category.

Should I Be Setting Up Automatic Bill Payments?

Setting up automatic bill payments can help ensure that you never miss a payment and can potentially improve your credit score. It can also save time by eliminating the need to manually pay bills each month.

What is a Mortgage Amortization Period?

The mortgage amortization period is the length of time it takes to pay off your mortgage in full. This can range from 10 to 30 years, depending on the terms of your mortgage.
